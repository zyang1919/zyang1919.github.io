My research focuses on developing Bayesian and likelihood-based statistical methodologies for complex biomedical data. I am particularly interested in modeling structural dependence, population heterogeneity, and informative observation mechanisms in longitudinal, survival, and high-dimensional genomic studies. My work integrates theoretical development, computational implementation in R, and applied collaboration in biostatistics and bioinformatics.

Current Research

Differential Methylation Regions Detection

DNA methylation plays an important role in regulating gene expression without changing the DNA sequence itself. Abnormal methylation patterns are frequently associated with cancer and other complex diseases. Detecting these abnormal patterns at the regional level is a central problem in modern bioinformatics.

In practice, methylation data are high-dimensional and noisy. Nearby CpG sites are spatially correlated, subjects exhibit biological heterogeneity, and signals are often weak. Standard site-by-site testing procedures tend to ignore regional structure, which reduces statistical power and complicates biological interpretation.

To address these challenges, I developed a Bayesian multistage Markov chain Monte Carlo (MMCMC) framework that adaptively partitions genomic regions and evaluates regional differences using Bayes factors. The methodology improves detection accuracy while preserving biological interpretability and is implemented in the R package mmcmcBayes.

Outcome-Dependent Follow-Up in Longitudinal Studies

In many longitudinal and electronic health record (EHR) studies, patient monitoring is not conducted at fixed intervals. Instead, follow-up intensity often depends on a patient’s recent health status or observed outcomes. This outcome-dependent follow-up mechanism introduces informative observation times.

Ignoring this dependence between the outcome process and the monitoring mechanism can lead to biased estimation and distorted inference, particularly when patients with worsening conditions are monitored more frequently.

I develop discrete-time hazard modeling frameworks that jointly characterize outcome trajectories and monitoring processes. The approach incorporates transition-based structures and machine learning methods to estimate patient risk levels that influence monitoring intensity, providing bias-corrected and interpretable inference for applied clinical research.

α-Mixture Models in Survival Analysis

Survival data often exhibit heterogeneity due to latent subpopulations and unobserved risk factors. Classical survival models assume homogeneous populations and may fail to capture complex hazard interactions.

Finite mixture models offer flexibility by representing survival distributions as weighted combinations of component distributions, but traditional formulations may lack interpretability.

I develop Bayesian α-mixture survival models that introduce a parameter controlling dependence between component hazards. This formulation provides greater modeling flexibility while maintaining tractable likelihood structures. The framework includes hierarchical extensions and posterior inference via Markov chain Monte Carlo, implemented in the R package alpmixBayes.
