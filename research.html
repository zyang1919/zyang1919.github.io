Current Research

Differentially Methylated Regions Detection

DNA methylation plays an important role in regulating gene expression without changing the DNA sequence itself. When methylation patterns go wrong, they are often linked to cancer and other complex diseases. Detecting these abnormal patterns, especially at the regional level, is a central problem in modern bioinformatics.

In practice, methylation data are high-dimensional and noisy. Nearby CpG sites are correlated, subjects can vary substantially, and signals are often weak. Standard site-by-site testing methods tend to ignore this regional structure, which can reduce statistical power and make biological interpretation harder.

To address these challenges, I developed a Bayesian multistage Markov chain Monte Carlo framework that adaptively partitions genomic regions and evaluates regional differences using Bayes factors. This approach improves detection accuracy while preserving biological interpretability. The methodology is implemented in the R package mmcmcBayes.

Outcome-Dependent Follow-Up in Longitudinal Studies

In many longitudinal and electronic health record (EHR) studies, patient monitoring is not conducted at fixed intervals. Instead, follow-up intensity often depends on a patient’s recent health status or observed outcomes. This outcome-dependent follow-up (ODFL) mechanism introduces informative observation times that violate the standard assumption of independent monitoring schedules.

Ignoring the dependence between the outcome process and the monitoring mechanism can lead to biased estimation and distorted inference. In particular, when patients with worsening conditions are observed more frequently, naive longitudinal models may overestimate disease progression or mischaracterize treatment effects.

To address this issue, I develop discrete-time hazard modeling frameworks that jointly characterize the outcome trajectory and the follow-up process. The monitoring mechanism is modeled using discrete-time transition structures, allowing the follow-up intensity to depend on previous outcomes. In addition, I incorporate machine learning methods to estimate patient risk levels that influence monitoring intensity. The goal is to reduce bias while keeping the models interpretable for applied clinical research.

Mixture Models in Survival Analysis (Collaborator)

Survival data arising from biomedical and clinical studies often exhibit heterogeneity due to unobserved subpopulations, varying risk profiles, or latent biological mechanisms. Classical survival models, such as the Cox proportional hazards model or parametric lifetime models, typically assume a homogeneous population structure and may fail to capture complex risk dynamics.

Finite mixture models provide a flexible framework for accommodating population heterogeneity by representing the survival distribution as a weighted combination of component distributions. However, traditional mixture formulations may lack interpretability and may not adequately model intermediate dependence structures between competing components.

To address these limitations, we develop Bayesian α-mixture survival models, which generalize classical mixture frameworks by introducing a parameter that controls the dependence and interaction between component hazards. This formulation allows for greater modeling flexibility while maintaining tractable likelihood structures. The methodology includes hierarchical extensions, posterior inference via Markov chain Monte Carlo, and computational implementation in R through the alpmixBayes package.
